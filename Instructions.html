Survey instructions

It is very important that you read the instructions carefully.

You will be shown 8 hypothetical scenarios about a potential exercise program. In each scenario, you will be asked to choose between exercise program A, or exercise program B. The two exercise programs will differ by location, price, session frequency, level of individualisation, involvement of carers, and chance of making a meaningful recovery.

For both scenarios, the program would start after discharge from hospital, be delivered in a group format by a qualified instructor and will last 12 weeks. Each exercise session will last between 30 - 50 minutes. Exercises may include walking, cycling on a stationary bike, using resistance bands, lifting weights, and balance exercises.

Below is a description of the different characteristics of the exercise program.

Location

This is where the exercise sessions will be delivered. When making your choice, consider how comfortable you are with using technology, the burden of transport, and the amount of support you would need to exercise. The options will be:

  • At home (connected to the exercise instructor via online software).
  • At a leisure centre.
  • Hybrid (a combination of sessions at home and at the leisure centre).

Price

Price is measured in British pounds (£), per month. As the program is 12 weeks long, the overall price will be equal to the monthly price multiplied by three. The options are:

  • Free.
  • £20 per month (£60 for the full 12-week program).
  • £40 per month (£120 for the full 12-week program).

Session frequency

This is how many exercise sessions you will take part in per week. The options are:

  • One per week.
  • Three per week.
  • Five per week.

Individualisation

This is how specific the exercises you are given are to your ability and goals. There are two options.

  • Targeted group exercises with stroke survivors of similar physical ability.
  • Generic group exercises with stroke survivors of mixed ability.

Carer involvement

This is what the carer receives as part of the program. There are three options.

  • Option one is that carers receive specific education sessions for stroke and exercise, as well as an opportunity to socialise with other carers of stroke survivors.
  • Option two is that carers take part in the exercise sessions with stroke survivors (at no extra cost), to support the stroke survivor they are caring for.
  • Option three is that carers are not involved with the program, which may provide some respite, while the stroke survivor is exercising.

Chance of meaningful improvement in recovery

No intervention can guarantee improvements for stroke survivors, but different types and intensities of rehabilitation can result in a better chance of achieving a positive outcome. A meaningful improvement is something that you would notice in your day-to-day life, for example, to walk up the stairs more easily, be able to use the toilet with more independence. It does not matter what the specific improvement is, as long as it is important and meaningful to you. The options are:

  • 20% chance of making a meaningful recovery. If 10 people took part in this program, 2 of those would achieve a meaningful improvement and 8 would not.
  • 50% chance of making a meaningful recovery. If 10 people took part in this program, 5 of those would achieve a meaningful improvement and 5 would not.
  • 80% chance of making a meaningful recovery. If 10 people took part in this program, 8 of those would achieve a meaningful improvement and 2 would not.

It is important to note that a 20% chance does not mean a 20% improvement. For example, it does not mean that you will be able to walk 20% faster after the program, just that the probability of you achieving your meaningful improvement is 20%.
